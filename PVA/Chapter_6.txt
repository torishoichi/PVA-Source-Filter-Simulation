Vocal resonance is the result of the interactions between voice source 
partials (frequencies) and vocal tract resonances. In general, we want 
approximate matches between our resonance tunings and those 
harmonics of the pitch being sung that are available for resonation. 
Harmonics may lose power more rapidly on the high side of a resonance 
frequency peak due to reactance (Titze). Since we have no control over 
the frequency location of the harmonics (they are determined by the pitch 
we are required to sing), the main factor that we can aﬀect in order to 
be resonant is the frequency location (tuning) of the /f_irst two vocal tract 
resonances and their resultant radiated formants. !eir tuning results 
from our vowel conception and intention and from expressive, aﬀective 
motivation. !e Western classical singer will want the frequency location 
of the clustered higher formants to be as stable as possible for consistency 
of overall timbre and Fach. However, the /f_irst two resonances can be tuned 
to amplify available harmonics when necessary.

Possible Resonance:Harmonic 
Interactions
!ere are four possible interactions between resonances and harmonics: 
(1) a resonance can allow a moving harmonic to pass through it (opening 
or closing the timbre); (2) a resonance can move across a stable harmonic 
(a change of vowel and possibly of acoustic register on the same pitch); 
(3) a resonance can be steadily retuned to track a moving harmonic (as in 
yelling, belting, or whooping), sometimes called formant tracking; and 
(4) a resonance can be “detuned” away from a harmonic (by raising fR1 
to stay above the sung pitch to remain in close timbre and avoid whoop 
timbre on close vowels by non-treble voices or belters; or by reducing 
higher formant clustering as a strategy in the upper treble voice range to 
eliminate the shrillness eﬀect of strong higher formants).

Open Timbre
When two or more harmonics lie at or below the /f_irst resonance, the 
timbre is described as open timbre  ( voce aperta  in Italian); the more 
harmonics below the /f_irst resonance, the more open the timbre. In other 
words, open timbre occurs when singing an octave or more below the /f_irst 
resonance of the vowel being sung. Open timbre might be described as 
bright, straightforward, clear, fresh, exposed, coming more directly out of 
the mouth, and—if low enough in pitch—buzzy. If unskillfully taken too 
high, open timbre can become blatant or spread as in a raw yell. Belting 
is a skillful variation of open timbre at higher pitches.  When the /f_irst 
resonance is tuned to the second harmonic, or conversely, as the second 
harmonic approaches the peak frequency of the /f_irst resonance, an fR1:2fo 
acoustic coupling occurs, which forms an especially strong form of open 
timbre, characterized by ringing clarity and power from enhanced higher 
spectral content. !is is such a strong acoustic coupling that we o/f_ten 
instinctively adjust for it when power and projection are needed. Novice 
singers tend to track this higher into yell timbre by an increasingly open, 
divergent vocal tract shaping.

Close Timbre
When the second harmonic crosses su#ciently above the /f_irst vocal tract 
resonance, the timbre is heard to close or turn over. !is crossing is the 
primary acoustic registration event of non-treble (bass, baritone, tenor) 
voices. Close timbre ( voce chiusa 1 in Italian) is variously described 
as seeming domed, tipped, smoother, rounder, more collected, or 
concentrated, above the palate or higher in sensation in the head, and 
somewhat less direct or exposed (covered). !is occurs when the sung 
pitch is less than an octave below the /f_irst resonance of the vowel being 
sung. It is a necessary component of Western classical upper voice 
training since, unlike other vocal styles, it enables the non-treble upper 
range and treble middle range to occur without loss of timbral depth or 
warmth. Turning over is accomplished by retaining depth (tube length), 
vowel shape, and relative vowel closeness—all of which maintain 
the /f_irst resonance frequency location—until the second harmonic 
has surpassed it. It is accompanied by some degree of passive vowel 
modi/f_ication or migration, a change in the vowel quality that is the 
result of maintaining the tube shape while the pitch and its harmonics 
move, in contrast to active modi/f_ication, which involves a deliberate 
shape change with a resultant retuning of resonances and resultant 
formants.

Yell and Whoop Frequency
!ese two resonance strategies are inherently strong acoustically and 
seem to occur innately and universally among humans. For example, 
when desiring to make a lot of sound at sporting events or enthusiastic 
celebrations, humans either yell or whoop. If done to excess, either can 
cause hyper-functional wear. However, whooping is the lower pressure, 
lower risk, vocally healthier of the two options. Cultural acceptability 
is another matter. “Bravo!” in whoop mode is not likely to catch on. 
Either strategy can be skillfully adapted for aesthetic, artistic use, taking 
advantage of their expressive associations “in the wild. ”
