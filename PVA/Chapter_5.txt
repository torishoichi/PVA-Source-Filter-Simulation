Vocal Tract
!e vocal tract is a tube that is open at one end (lips) and mostly closed 
at the other (glottis). As mentioned above, that makes it a quarter-wave 
resonator, with a primary resonance or standing wave at the frequency of 
a sound wave that is four times as long as the vocal tract. Such resonators 
also have resonances at higher frequencies, whose standing wavelengths 
within the vocal tract are odd-numbered multiples of one-fourth: 3/4, 
5/4, 7/4, and so on, all of which have nodal and anti-nodal segments 
at opposite ends of the tube ( Figure 4.1). Hard-walled resonators—like 
brass instruments—will only resonate partials at rather speci/f_ic, narrowed 
frequency locations. Being a so/f_t-walled, shapeable tube, the vocal tract 
has resonances with frequency bandwidths that are wider and less sharply 
tuned than other quarter-wave instruments. !is enables the vocal tract 
to reinforce source partials that are merely in the vicinity of its resonance 
frequency peaks. Frequencies that fall midway between widely spaced 
vocal tract resonances will be signi/f_icantly weakened, if not suppressed 
altogether.
