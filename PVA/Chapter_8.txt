Familiarity with Vowel Turning
A familiarity with the locations, sounds, and sensations of vowel turning 
is necessary. Since letting the voice turn over—rather than yelling—is 
not instinctive, knowing when and where to expect vowels to turn over 
can be very helpful to the young singer. If vowels do not turn over where 
expected, one of two things has happened to keep the /f_irst formant 
above the second harmonic: the vowel has been opened (a sometimes 
reasonable but pitch- and diction-limited strategy) or the larynx has been 
raised (rarely a good strategy in classical genre), or most likely, both. 
Experiencing vowel turning on the close vowels [i] and [u], which close 
well below the traditional lower passaggio location, can be instructive. 
Close [o] and [e] turn at about the traditionally understood location of the 
primo passaggio, at the low end of the zona di passaggio. !eir depth and 
closeness are relatively easy to monitor and coach. An untrained singer 
will tend to open [o] toward [ ʌ] and [e] toward [ ɛ] rather than allowing 
the turn. Resonant, in&ective speech sounds that loop above, gliding up 
and over the predicted pitch of turning and ease back down through it are 
easy initial ways to explore vowel turning. If the yell instinct is stubborn, 
suggesting depth, thinking a slightly closer vowel and an internalizing 
aﬀect or direction (sobby; in, down and out) while ascending, are o/f_ten 
fruitful strategies. Avoiding chin tuck, and instead, allowing the head to 
tip slightly back and up as the warmer, darker “ under-vowel” tone color

the TMJ facilitates the turn as well. !e eventual actual goal is to maintain 
rather than change tube shape while looping up, allowing the acoustic,

Vowel Modiﬁcation Revisited
!e question of vowel modi/f_ication vs. vowel integrity  or “purity” has 
been much debated across the history of vocal pedagogy. Both concepts 
require yet elude clear de/f_inition, since human perception is involved. 
Given our new understanding that, to human perception, sound is a 
blend of inherent, frequency-dependent spectral tone colors, which 
therefore necessarily change with every pitch change, and the fact that the 
tone color contributions of individual frequencies increase and decrease 
as harmonics move into and through vocal tract resonance peaks, it 
becomes clear that vowel migration is inevitable. !e concept of vowel 
modi/f_ication has historically ranged from conscious vocal tract shape 
changes with the intent of producing a modi/f_ied vowel timbre to outright 
vowel substitution. !e concept of passive vowel migration introduced 
here points out that some degree of vowel shi/f_t will dependably, inevitably 
occur when the vowel shaping of the vocal tract tube is kept stable while 
the pitch (with its set of potential harmonics) is raised, as harmonics 
change in tone color and intensity as they move through resonances. !is 
kind of vowel change will feel to the singer like they are maintaining the 
vowel—at least in shape—while allowing a subtle migration within the 
same family, or on the same “side of the street” in terms of front-back 
designation. What all teachers presumably want to avoid is the perception 
of a vowel distortion. !is occurs when, for example, an open back vowel, 
such as [ɔ] takes on some of the quality of a mixed vowel such as [œ]. !is 
is more indicative of mistaken tongue shape (moving the dorsum-palatal-
narrowing too far forward) or excess tongue tension than of the acoustics 
of turning over, and does not result from maintaining the normal speech 
shape of [ɔ] over the fR1:2fo crossing, which should migrate instead in the 
direction of [ʊ].

Along with the primary turn, these secondary timbral events are

exploited for artistic purposes, since their passive timbral migrations
