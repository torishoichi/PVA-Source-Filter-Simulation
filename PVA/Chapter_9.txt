Perceptions of Turning Over
!e reader may wonder, “When a voice turns over—when 2fo rises through 
fR1—what does it sound like to the audience? And what does it sound and 
feel like to the singer?”
First it should be noted that this question is especially signi/f_icant for 
non-treble voices to access their upper range, since all vowels will have 
turned over in the octave below G4, and for deep voices as early as by D4. 
And yet there are parallel percepts for the lower passaggio of treble voices 
in the same range where transitioning from laryngeal mechanism one to 
mechanism two commences—the range ca. between the bass and treble 
clefs. Furthermore, the answer is complicated by the fact that it will vary 
somewhat from vowel to vowel, from voice type to voice type, and from 
whether the cause is active or passive vowel modi/f_ication. Finally, since 
subjective, sensory perceptions are individual, predictive descriptions of 
perception are inherently variable and therefore cannot be in&exibly 
described. All that said, the following descriptions and pedagogic 
opinions—based on historic pedagogic terminology descriptive of 
procedural knowledge, personal experience, anecdote, impression, and 
student response, though not subjected to the rigorous testing of scienti/f_ic 
methodology—are typical and, in the author’s experience, reliably useful.

Perception of Tonal Sensation
!e very term “turning over” is descriptive of the acoustic sensation. 
!ere is a sense of the top of the tonal sensation arching, internalizing, 
rounding, tipping or rolling up above the palate and into the head toward 
the front, in other words, “turning over” and reverberating in the head 
more as opposed to coming directly out of the mouth as in open timbre. 
Care should be taken to avoid an instinctive postural chin-tucking in an 
attempt to precipitate or assist this turning percept. At the same time, 
there may be an oppositional sensation of the “underbelly” of the tone 
settling through a relaxed neck into the body and rolling down and under. 
!is latter aspect counters the reaching up and out sensation typical of the 
throat constriction of a yell. Together these sensations might seem like the

high as the vowel and pitch will allow to maintain the “bene/f_icial 
narrowing” needed for the interactive acoustic feedback of convergence.
Perception by Voice Type and 
Vowel
!e lighter the voice type, usually the lighter the turn and modi/f_ication 
will sound. !e lower, heavier, or deeper the voice type, the more 
noticeable the turn will likely sound. Leggiero tenors may have rather 
subtle timbral shi/f_ts, while basses, deep baritones, and dramatic tenors 
tend to have more obvious, even dramatic shi/f_ts. Since this passaggio is 
low in treble voices and at lower pressure levels, its eﬀects are noticeable 
but more subtle. !e turn is also more obvious on open vowels, especially 
open back vowels such as [ ɔ] and [ɑ]. !e stronger the ring component, 
if freely gotten and sensed high in the vertical “back room” through the

!is may be due to a su#ciently strong singer’s formant or to an eﬀective
